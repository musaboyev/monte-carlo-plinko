Why this game is unfair

At first glance, Plinko looks completely random. The ball bounces left or right, lands in a slot, and sometimes pays well. But underneath that randomness is a precise mathematical structure designed so that the house wins in the long run.

Binomial distribution on a Plinko board

Each time the ball hits a peg, it has two possible directions: left or right. If the board is fair, each happens with probability 50%. After several rows, the final slot depends on how many times the ball moved right. This follows a binomial distribution, which means middle slots are much more likely than the rare edge slots.

Expected Value (EV)

The Expected Value tells us the average result per game over the long run. It is found by multiplying each payout by its probability and adding everything together. In this game, the low-paying middle slots happen often, while the high-paying edge slots happen rarely. That makes the average result negative for the player.

Monte Carlo simulation

A Monte Carlo simulation runs the game many times and tracks the average outcome. In the graph above, the blue line shows the simulated average profit per game, and the red dashed line shows the theoretical EV. As the number of simulations increases, the blue line moves closer to the red line.

Why the house wins

In the short run, anything can happen. A player might get lucky and win. But in the long run, randomness averages out and results converge to the Expected Value. That is why the game may feel fair at first, but mathematically it is not: the house edge guarantees an average loss over time.
